II

Ethical Theories and Frameworks

Trust as the architecture of markets — and the philosophical lenses (deontology, consequentialism, virtue ethics) financial professionals use to reason their way through ambiguity.

Chapter Outcomes

  1. Understand the importance of trust and client protection as the framework ensuring sustainability of financial markets, and recognize systemic consequences when these foundations are compromised.
  2. Identify ethical theories that serve as a roadmap for delivering financial advice and services.
  3. Apply philosophical perspectives to guide ethical behaviors and the decision-making processes of financial services professionals.
  4. Demonstrate the practical application of ethics in the advisor-client relationship, including strategies for navigating ambiguity and managing conflicts of interest.
  5. Evaluate ethical reasoning as a strategic and moral compass that reinforces trust and fosters positive client engagements.
Section i

Trust → Theories → Decision

Use as your anchor

Chapter 2 builds the conceptual scaffolding you will use through every later chapter. Mutual trust deepens into reciprocal trust. Three classical ethical theories — duty-based, outcome-based, character-based — provide complementary lenses. And Johnson's Five-Step Model gives you a practical procedure for moving from ethical recognition to defensible action.

01
Mutual Trust
The belief that each party in a transaction will act in good faith. The minimum precondition for any market to function. Without it, transaction costs explode and the market freezes.
02
Reciprocal Trust
Beyond passive belief: each party actively upholds their own responsibilities. A dynamic, ongoing commitment to ethical conduct, transparency, and the mutual fulfilment of obligations.
03
Stakeholder Reciprocity
Recognising the interconnected nature of financial systems — clients, firms, regulators, society. Avoiding harm AND intentionally creating value and fairness across the system.
04
Deontology
Duty-based ethics. Rooted in Kant. Action evaluated by adherence to rules, duties, and obligations. Anchors fiduciary duty, confidentiality, and fair-treatment standards.
05
Consequentialism
Outcome-based ethics. Rooted in Bentham and Mill (utilitarianism). Action evaluated by results — the greatest good for the greatest number.
06
Virtue Ethics
Character-based ethics. Rooted in Aristotle. Action evaluated by whether it expresses honesty, prudence, courage, integrity — virtues cultivated through habit and practice.

Johnson's Five-Step Ethical Decision-Making Model

Step 1 — Recognize the issue

Detect ethical signals — stakeholder vulnerability, injustice, breaches of trust. Ask: Is someone being harmed? Are core values challenged? Could this set a troubling precedent? Failing to recognise an ethical issue produces morally negligent outcomes.

Step 2 — Gather pertinent information

Collect legal considerations, stakeholder perspectives, contextual factors, and factual data. Consult experts, challenge biases, scrutinise sources. Ask: What are the relevant laws, policies, norms? Who are the stakeholders? What information might be missing or distorted?

Step 3 — Apply ethical reasoning frameworks

Use multiple lenses to avoid moral myopia. Utilitarianism: What are the consequences of each option? Deontology: What duties or principles must be upheld? Virtue ethics: Which choice best reflects integrity and character?

Step 4 — Choose, then test the decision

Subject the chosen course to ethical scrutiny. Apply the Public Disclosure Test (Would I be comfortable if this were publicly known?), the Role Model Test (Would I advise others to act the same way?), and the Empathy Test (How would I feel on the receiving end?).

Step 5 — Implement and reflect

Execute transparently and with respect for affected parties. After: reflect on results — what went well, what went poorly, what to do differently next time. Self-reflection fosters ethical maturity and continuous improvement.
