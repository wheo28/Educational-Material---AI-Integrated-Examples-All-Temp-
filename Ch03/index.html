III

Ethical Decision-Making in the Financial Services Profession

Three theories. Seven steps. Seven biases. A practical roadmap for moving from "I sense something is wrong" to a documented, defensible decision — illustrated by FTX, Carter, Thompson, and Apex.

Chapter Outcomes

  1. Apply ethical frameworks (deontology, consequentialism, virtue ethics) along with foundational values like beneficence, autonomy, and justice to guide ethical decision-making.
  2. Utilize a structured, step-by-step process for integrating ethical principles into day-to-day professional practice.
  3. Analyze real-world case examples to understand the practical application of ethical decision-making.
  4. Identify and manage behavioral biases — including overconfidence, confirmation bias, and herd mentality — that can compromise ethical judgment.
  5. Employ ethics and decision-making bias checklists to enhance self-awareness and maintain ethical standards in high-stakes financial environments.
Section i

Three lenses · Seven steps · Seven biases

Use as your anchor

Chapter 3 takes the philosophical theories of Chapter 2 and translates them into something you can actually use on a Monday morning. The seven-step process gives you a procedure. The seven biases give you a watchlist. Together they form the chapter's central tool: a defensible, repeatable way to reason your way through ambiguous ethical situations.

01
Recognise the dilemma
Identify whether the situation involves potential harm, fairness issues, or conflicts between values. Watch for red flags: conflicts of interest, KYC/AML breaches, suitability gaps, data privacy risks, missing documentation, high-pressure tactics.
02
Gather facts
Collect relevant data: stakeholder interests, legal obligations, organisational policies, client goals and risk tolerance, market conditions. Quantitative AND qualitative — partial information produces partial ethics.
03
Evaluate alternatives
Use deontology, consequentialism, and virtue ethics to weigh courses of action. "Am I meeting my duties to the client? What are the broader consequences? Does this reflect principled professional behaviour?"
04
Decide & communicate
Select the most ethical course; communicate transparently to clients (principles + data), colleagues (team briefings), and leadership (long-term trust, compliance, sustainability). Document the rationale.
05
Implement
Put the decision into practice through organisational processes. Approval procedures, cross-departmental collaboration, communication strategy, oversight mechanisms.
06
Review & improve
Post-decision: how well did it work? Was the process effective? Then feedback and continuous improvement — gather input, run training/case reviews, capture lessons learned.

The seven cognitive biases that compromise ethical judgement

Overconfidence

The financial professional who overestimates their expertise ignores warnings or contradictory evidence and stubbornly sticks to a specific conclusion. (Jane at FTX: trusted her ability to assess crypto exchanges despite limited expertise. John Carter: assumed familiarity with SEC rules would let him structure 'clean' insider trades.)

Confirmation bias

Selectively seeking out information supporting your preferred outcome and dismissing contradictory evidence. (Sarah Thompson on the variable annuity: focused on guaranteed income, only skimmed the 2.5% annual fee and 10-year surrender period.)

Availability heuristic

Giving disproportionate weight to recent or vivid examples. A loan officer who fixates on a recent high-profile bankruptcy may adopt overly cautious lending practices even when statistical default rates are low.

Groupthink (herd mentality)

When no one is willing to voice dissent, the group rushes to consensus without adequate scrutiny. "Everyone seems to approve, so it must be fine." (Apex Wealth: junior analysts Mark and Priya silenced; Jane at FTX: "everyone is on FTX.")

Halo effect

Letting a positive impression of a person or institution skew judgement. An advisor overestimates a client's creditworthiness because of polished demeanour; advisors trusted FTX because of SBF's reputation as a 'genius.'

Self-justification

Holding on to a previous decision out of fear that changing course will damage personal or team reputation. The longer a flawed decision runs, the harder reversal becomes.

Emotion-driven judgement

Anger, empathy, fear, or gut feelings overriding rational analysis. A strong emotional reaction can prompt a hasty recommendation that deviates from objective data.
