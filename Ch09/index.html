Insider Trading & Market Manipulation

From McDermott's pillow talk to Sarao's Flash Crash — how MNPI, spoofing, wash trading, and social-media-driven herding strike at the heart of market integrity, and what compliance frameworks must do to detect them.

Chapter Outcomes

  1. Articulate definitions, forms, and characteristics of insider trading and market manipulation, and explain their detrimental effects on financial markets and public trust.
  2. Describe the legal frameworks, key enforcement actions, and roles of oversight agencies responsible for prevention.
  3. Analyse how insider trading and market manipulation undermine fairness, transparency, and integrity in markets and client relationships.
  4. Interpret real-world case studies to extract practical lessons for ethical awareness and professional judgement.
  5. Identify psychological and behavioural factors that contribute to insider trading and market manipulation.
  6. Explain the importance of a proactive compliance culture in preventing insider trading.
Section i

MNPI · Manipulation typologies · Behavioural drivers

Use as your anchor

Chapter 9 distinguishes two related but distinct violations: insider trading (using material non-public information) and market manipulation (artificially distorting price or volume signals). The chapter traces both through their legal foundations — Rule 10b-5, Section 9(a) — and through specific behavioural typologies: pump-and-dump, spoofing, wash trading, quote stuffing, social-media-driven herding.

01
MNPI — Material Non-Public Information
Insider trading is the buying or selling of securities by someone possessing MNPI. Three-part test: material? non-public? obtained through a duty of trust/confidence? Prohibited under Section 10(b) and Rule 10b-5 of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934.
02
Tipper-Tippee Liability
Established by Dirks v. SEC. Recipients of insider information (tippees) can be held liable if they know the tip was provided in breach of a fiduciary duty. The chain extends: source → tipper → tippee → tippee's tippee. Each link can carry liability.
03
Disclose-or-Abstain Rule
Established by SEC v. Texas Gulf Sulphur Co. Anyone in possession of MNPI must either disclose it publicly or abstain from trading. There is no third option — "trade carefully and don't tell anyone" is not a permissible choice.
04
Pump-and-Dump
Disseminate false or misleading information about thinly-traded micro-cap stocks to inflate price, then offload at the top. 2018 SEC case: a network using social media generated $100M+ in illicit gains. Digital platforms have made this scalable.
05
Spoofing
Submit large orders with the intent to cancel before execution, creating false impressions of demand or supply. Triggers algorithmic and human responses that the spoofer exploits. Navinder Sarao's spoofing contributed to the 2010 Flash Crash.
06
Wash Trading & Quote Stuffing
Wash trading: simultaneous buy/sell of the same asset to fabricate volume and signal liquidity. Quote stuffing: rapid order submission and cancellation to slow rivals' trading systems. Both common in crypto venues and the focus of recent CFTC enforcement.

Behavioural drivers and prevention

Digital Disinhibition and Anonymity

Online environments lower social inhibitions. Anonymity removes face-to-face accountability. Anonymous forums make coordinated manipulation (or just impulsive insider tips) feel less weighty than they are. Surveillance must extend to digital channels — internal chat, encrypted messaging, social media — not just trade tape.

Moral Disengagement and Ethical Fading

When the harm is diffuse ("the market" rather than a specific victim), moral salience fades. Traders rationalise: "everyone does it," "the market absorbs it," "no one is really hurt." The fix: structured ethical reminders (training, attestations, decision logs) that re-introduce moral salience at decision points.

Normalisation of Deviance in High-Pressure Environments

Repeated minor rule-bending becomes routine. Each incremental violation feels small relative to the prior baseline. By the time the conduct is clearly wrong, the actor cannot remember when it became so. Defence: independent review that resets the baseline (random sampling, supervisory walks, exception escalation).

Temporal Discounting and Short-Termism

Future consequences feel less salient than immediate gains. The bonus arriving in 60 days outweighs the regulator arriving in 18 months. Defence: clawback provisions, deferred compensation, post-trade review, and surveillance that reaches back across long windows.

Behavioural Interventions and Prevention

Restricted/watch lists. Pre-clearance for insider trades. Information barriers ("Chinese walls"). Real-time surveillance and analytics. Mandatory training with attestation. Documentation standards. Escalation and whistleblower channels with anti-retaliation. All combined: a compliance culture, not just compliance procedures.
