The Evolving Role of Ethics in a Digital Age

Where Chapter 14 mapped the technologies, Chapter 15 builds the governance. The SR 11-7 five-step control loop — Policy → Risk Assessment → Implementation → Monitoring → Evidence — turned into actionable practice through global standards (EU AI Act, DORA, UK Consumer Duty, CFPB §1033) and one cautionary eKYC case.

Chapter Outcomes

  1. Explain how a structured and consistent evaluative lens aligns technology adoption at the firm level with global compliance rules and regulations.
  2. Identify practice risks and describe how risks can be converted into defined controls to address audits and quality-of-advice assurance checks.
  3. Outline a practical approach for financial services firms to enhance ethics compliance processes to ensure technology adoption aligns with organisational goals and regulatory expectations.
  4. Assess how technology adoption, guided by ethical compliance processes, supports the broader objectives of financial services firms.
Section i

The five-step control loop · Five global standards

Use as your anchor

Chapter 15 turns the technology landscape from Chapter 14 into governance. The SR 11-7 control loop is the analytical spine: Policy → Risk Assessment → Implementation → Monitoring → Evidence — a cyclical model that translates regulatory principles into daily operations. Around it, the global regulatory ecosystem: EU AI Act, EU DORA, UK Consumer Duty, US CFPB §1033, and the US Model Risk Governance Framework (SR 11-7 itself).

01
Policy
Foundational step. Establishes overarching duties and standards drawing from regulatory expectations: fiduciary responsibilities, best-interest obligations, fairness principles, honesty and transparency commitments. Clearly documented, aligned with industry guidelines, regularly updated for emerging technologies and regulatory changes. The ethical compass for all subsequent steps.
02
Risk Assessment
Systematically identify potential pitfalls of technology adoption: client harm (biased algorithms producing discriminatory outcomes), market integrity failures (manipulative trading via automated systems), institutional safety/soundness threats (cybersecurity vulnerabilities). Comprehensive scenario analysis, stakeholder input, quantitative modelling. Prioritise by likelihood and impact.
03
Implementation
Build on identified risks: deploy concrete controls, assign defined roles and responsibilities, establish operational processes to mitigate risks. Examples: access controls, algorithmic audits, third-party vendor due-diligence protocols. Ensure technology integrations are seamless, scalable, and aligned with policy objectives.
04
Monitoring
Verify ongoing effectiveness. Use Key Risk Indicators (KRIs) to flag potential issues early, Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) to measure success against benchmarks, regular testing through simulations or audits. Continuous oversight enables real-time adjustments and detects deviations from expected performance.
05
Evidence
Compile and maintain artifacts that provide tangible proof of compliance and diligence — material regulators and auditors can review. Examples: system logs, model validation reports, "model cards" detailing AI/ML system behaviours and limitations, meeting minutes documenting oversight discussions and decisions.
06
Why a control loop, not a checklist
The five steps form a dynamic, repeatable system that REFRESHES as new technologies are adopted. Each step feeds the next; each evidence-collection cycle informs the next policy update. The cyclical structure ensures regulatory compliance keeps pace with technology innovation rather than chasing it after each failure.

The global standards a Ch.15 advisor must know

EU AI Act (effective August 1, 2024)

Tiered risk-based regulatory framework for AI systems. Various obligations gradually implemented through 2025 and 2026. Establishes categories: unacceptable-risk (banned), high-risk (heavily regulated, including most financial services AI), limited-risk (transparency obligations), minimal-risk. Extraterritorial scope; affects any firm whose AI outputs are used in the EU.

EU DORA (Digital Operational Resilience Act, January 2025)

Standardises ICT risk management and incident reporting across EU financial institutions. Built around five pillars: ICT risk management, incident reporting, digital operational resilience testing, ICT third-party risk management, information sharing. Concentration-risk and exit-plan-drill expectations.

UK Consumer Duty

FCA's outcomes-focused regulation. Firms must "act to deliver good outcomes for retail customers." Four outcomes: products and services; price and value; consumer understanding; consumer support. Ongoing duty — not just at point of sale. Particularly relevant to AI-driven product recommendations and digital service journeys.

US CFPB §1033 (Open Banking Rule)

Section 1033 of Dodd-Frank. CFPB rule mandating consumer-permissioned data access. Standards for secure third-party authorisation, data security, scope minimisation, audit rights, revocation pathways. The US analogue to PSD2 — but with distinct architecture for consent lifecycle and purpose limitation.

US Model Risk Governance — SR 11-7

Federal Reserve guidance on model risk management. The foundational framework for the five-step control loop. Banks must (1) define policies for model use, (2) identify risks (bias, misuse), (3) implement controls for development and validation, (4) monitor performance through back-testing and challenger models, (5) maintain evidence (validation reports, governance committee minutes). Capital One 2019 case: regulators found weaknesses at steps 3 and 4.

Third-party risk + Operational resilience + Cross-border data

OCC Third-Party Risk Management Handbook + 2023 Interagency Third-Party Guidance: vendor oversight as the firm's responsibility. FCA Operational Resilience Policy Statement (PS21/3): impact-tolerance and exit-plan drills. EU-US Data Privacy Framework + Standard Contractual Clauses: cross-border data-transfer mechanisms post-Schrems II.
