XIV

Emerging Trends

Five technologies are reshaping the profession — AI/ML, RegTech/SupTech, DLT, Open Banking, and Cloud/SaaS. Each creates new value AND new vulnerabilities. The behavioral dynamics around trust, overconfidence, resistance, risk perception, and digital literacy decide which firms make the transition.

Chapter Outcomes

  1. Identify the five core technologies reshaping financial services: AI/ML, RegTech and SupTech, Distributed Ledger Technology and tokenization, Open Banking and APIs, Cloud/SaaS platforms.
  2. Evaluate the successes and limitations of technology adoption, balancing efficiency gains with regulatory, operational, and ethical constraints.
  3. Analyse the behavioural dynamics that influence technology adoption — trust, overconfidence, resistance to change, risk perception, digital literacy.
  4. Integrate insights from technology adoption and human behaviour to understand how innovation creates new opportunities AND new vulnerabilities.
Section i

Five technologies · Five behavioural dynamics

Use as your anchor

Chapter 14 maps the technological surface of the profession: five interconnected technologies that drive nearly every consumer-facing innovation, and five behavioural dynamics that determine whether each technology actually gets adopted. The chapter's core insight: launching a digital product is not the same as transforming an organisation — agility, user experience, and strategic capacity decide the outcome.

01
AI/ML — beyond chatbots and robo-advisors
Pattern detection in vast unstructured data; deep learning's neural networks for predictive accuracy. Applications: personalised advice, credit scoring (with fairness/bias concerns), fraud detection, predictive analytics anticipating life events. Robo-advisors (Betterment, Wealthfront, KakaoPay Securities) democratised access while creating the algorithmic-trust problem.
02
RegTech and SupTech
Regulatory Technology (firms automating their compliance) and Supervisory Technology (regulators automating their oversight). The instant fraud-confirmation text, the new-device authentication prompt — these are the consumer surfaces of a much larger compliance infrastructure shift.
03
DLT & Tokenization
Distributed ledger technology = decentralised method for recording and synchronising data across networks. Blockchain is one specific form. Tokenisation: digitally representing traditional assets (bonds, equities, real estate) on a distributed ledger. EIB's €100M Ethereum bond (2021) demonstrated faster cheaper settlement potential — but custody, interoperability, and durability remain barriers.
04
Open Banking & APIs
Regulatory mandate AND market-driven framework that shifts financial-data control from institutions to consumers. APIs replace screen-scraping with auditable, transparent data movement. PSD2 in EU; UK's Open Banking Initiative; Korea's MyData. "Standardised road system" enabling AI/ML to function across fragmented ecosystems.
05
Cloud Computing & SaaS
What consumers see: smoother apps, faster online banking, new digital features. What clients don't see: re-architected operational backbone, shared infrastructure with vendor dependency, new categories of resilience and exit-plan risk.
06
Five behavioural dynamics
Trust & transparency (German robo-advisor research: clients need to understand HOW). Overconfidence in technology (banks cutting AML staff on AI promise — fines followed). Resistance to change (PSD2 adoption lag despite legal readiness). Risk perceptions (DLT tarred by crypto association). Digital literacy gaps (older/less-fluent clients silently excluded).
· · ·

Behavioural adoption dynamics — what they look like in practice

Trust & Transparency

Adoption depends not on technical accuracy alone but on perceived fairness, clarity, and explainability. German research on robo-advisors: potential clients hesitate unless they understand HOW portfolios are built and managed. When firms provide transparent explanations and human-like rationales, adoption rates increase. Lesson: transparent design is itself a competitive feature, not just an ethical add-on.

Overconfidence in Technology

Mirror-image risk to trust. Firm leadership may assume cloud migration eliminates internal oversight; that AI-driven compliance replaces human judgement. Numerous global banks adopted ML-based AML systems AND cut staff — expecting the algorithm to catch everything. Regulators penalised them. Automation cannot replace accountability. Overconfidence undermines resilience and regulatory trust as much as resistance does.

Resistance to Change

Even with regulatory frameworks and technical safeguards in place, consumers and firms may cling to familiar practices. UK Open Banking: many consumers reluctant to share data with third parties. EBA 2020 survey: substantial preference for traditional logins over API-based authentication under PSD2. Internal resistance: European compliance officers sceptical of RegTech, fearing automation would undermine their professional judgement. Cultural acceptance lags regulatory and technical readiness.

Risk Perceptions and Volatility

Reputational bias from association. DLT often perceived through crypto's volatility lens. Japanese pilot programs for DLT-based securities settlement faced retail-investor scepticism — even with strong institutional safeguards. Perception outweighs technical merit. The behavioural lesson: how a technology is FRAMED determines adoption as much as how it WORKS.

Digital Literacy Gaps

Younger, tech-savvy clients embrace robo-advisors, mobile-first platforms, aggregator apps. Older and less-fluent consumers struggle with passwords, MFA, app navigation. US research on budgeting apps: older clients hesitate not from distrust but from discomfort with digital interfaces. This produces an UNEVEN distribution of innovation benefits — questions of inclusiveness, fairness, and access. The chapter's link to Chapter 11's vulnerable-client themes.

Why all five dynamics matter together

Each dynamic operates simultaneously across different stakeholders. A firm that solves trust without addressing literacy gaps creates products only some clients can use. A firm that solves resistance through marketing while overconfident in its technology produces vulnerable scale. The interaction effects matter: behavioural design must address all five dynamics across employees, clients, regulators, and the broader market.
