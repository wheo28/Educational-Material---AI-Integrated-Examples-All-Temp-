VI

From Misconduct to Oversight

A century of financial regulation is the institutional memory of a century of financial misconduct. From the 1929 crash to Madoff to Wells Fargo — every modern compliance rule traces to a specific failure that came before.

Chapter Outcomes

  1. Understand the historical development of financial regulations and the significance of regulations in driving ethical and compliant practices.
  2. Analyse the evolution of regulatory frameworks behind current rules and anticipate potential future regulatory changes.
  3. Identify landmark cases and regulatory milestones that have shaped the financial services profession.
  4. Evaluate lessons from regulatory breakdowns and describe the catastrophic consequences of oversight failures and ethical misconduct.
  5. Apply insights from historical cases to foster a culture of proactive compliance and continuous risk assessment.
  6. Describe the roles of regulatory bodies — SEC and FINRA — in maintaining professional standards and accountability.
  7. Develop a principled approach to financial advising grounded in historical awareness, regulatory understanding, and ethical integrity.
Section i

Five eras of financial regulation

Use as your anchor

The history of financial regulation is not a smooth progression — it is a series of reactive shocks. Crisis exposes failure; failure produces reform; reform leaves new gaps for the next innovation to exploit. Chapter 6 traces this cycle through five distinct eras, each defined by what went wrong and what was built in response.

01
Pre-20th century foundations
Banking guilds, Renaissance codes of conduct (Medici), early consumer-protection statutes. Self-regulation and reputation-based trust dominated; formal federal oversight was minimal.
02
1929 → modern regulation (1933–1940)
Stock-market crash and Great Depression triggered the foundational federal architecture: Securities Act 1933, Securities Exchange Act 1934 (creating the SEC), Glass-Steagall Banking Act 1933 (and FDIC), Investment Company Act 1940, Investment Advisers Act 1940.
03
Post-WWII expansion (1940–1980)
Institutional development. Securities Investor Protection Corporation (SIPC) created 1970. Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA) 1974 — fiduciary standards for pensions. Self-regulatory organisation maturation (NASD, later FINRA).
04
Deregulation & crises (1980–2000)
Savings & Loan crisis. Glass-Steagall partially dismantled (1999 Gramm-Leach-Bliley). Long-Term Capital Management collapse 1998. Tech bubble. The deregulation era leaves the gaps that fill the next era's caseload.
05
Post-2000 reform & globalisation (2000–present)
Sarbanes-Oxley 2002 (Enron). Dodd-Frank 2010 (2008 GFC). Basel III. MiFID II. CFPB and SEC Office of the Whistleblower (Madoff). Reg BI 2019. Now: cryptocurrency, AI governance, ESG, cross-border data.

Five lessons from regulatory failure

Inadequate oversight catalyses market harm

Madoff continued his scheme for years despite credible whistleblower warnings (Harry Markopolos). The SEC's failure to act was as catastrophic as the fraud itself. Lesson: oversight quality matters more than oversight existence — a regulator that doesn't respond to credible warnings is, functionally, no regulator at all.

Ethical lapses undermine trust and threaten markets

Enron's collapse erased shareholder value, dissolved Arthur Andersen, and destroyed thousands of pensions. The damage extended far beyond Enron: investors questioned every audited financial statement in corporate America. Lesson: ethical failure at one major institution radiates outward, eroding trust across the entire system.

Deregulation without safeguards amplifies risk

Glass-Steagall's partial dismantling enabled new conflicts of interest that the 2008 crisis would expose. Innovation outran the oversight infrastructure built for older products. Lesson: deregulation requires compensating risk controls — not the absence of controls.

Consumer protection is paramount

Wells Fargo's 3.5+ million unauthorised accounts directly harmed customers through unauthorised fees, damaged credit scores, and (in some cases) legal action. The CFPB, OCC, and LA City Attorney levied a $185 million fine. Lesson: when sales pressure overrides client welfare, no level of fines can fully repair the trust damage.

Whistleblower protections enhance accountability

Madoff's exposure prompted Dodd-Frank to create the SEC Office of the Whistleblower with explicit protections AND financial incentives for reporting fraud. The post-Madoff infrastructure recognises that internal voices are the most reliable early-warning system. Lesson: protect and incentivise the people who tell the truth before everyone else does.
