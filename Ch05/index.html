Regulatory Frameworks

From the 1933 Truth in Securities Act to Dodd-Frank — the regulatory architecture that shapes every recommendation a financial professional makes, and the standards (fiduciary vs. best-interest) that define duty.

Chapter Outcomes

  1. Understand the role of financial services regulations in ensuring integrity, transparency, and fairness within financial markets.
  2. Identify key regulatory standards relevant to financial services professionals — best-interest requirements, transparent fees, professional conduct standards.
  3. Explain how regulations promote consumer well-being by fostering trust and accountability in advisory practices.
  4. Describe the roles of regulatory bodies — SEC, FINRA, DOL, FinCEN, CFPB, the CFP Board, and others — in overseeing compliance and enforcement.
  5. Evaluate the impact of regulatory frameworks on building professional integrity and long-term, trust-based client relationships.
Section i

Three purposes · Two standards · Many regulators

Use as your anchor

Chapter 5 maps the full regulatory landscape a financial advisor operates in. Regulations exist to ensure market stability, consumer protection, and economic growth. Within that frame, two standards define duty (fiduciary vs. best-interest), six US laws form the core regulatory backbone, and a layered network of federal regulators, state regulators, and certification bodies enforces conduct.

01
Market stability
Regulations require capital buffers, risk management, and ethical standards to prevent market failures. The 2008 GFC remains the case-study evidence for what happens when these break down.
02
Consumer protection
Mandated transparency, accountability, and fair practices — KYC, AML, suitability, fee disclosure. Regulators enforce rules so clients can trust the system even when they can't verify every detail.
03
Economic growth
Compliant, trustworthy markets attract capital. Proactive compliance is not an obstacle; it is a strategic advantage for both firms and individual advisors.
04
Fiduciary standard
Highest duty of care. Act SOLELY in the client's best interest. Avoid OR fully disclose AND mitigate conflicts. Applies to RIAs, CFP®, CFA®, and ERISA fiduciaries. Fee-only or fee-based models are favoured.
05
Best-interest standard
Act in the client's best interest while considering firm and professional interests. Disclose conflicts but not necessarily eliminate them. Applies to broker-dealers (Reg BI), insurance agents (NAIC), under FINRA Rule 2111.
06
Continuing education
Both federal regulators and certification bodies require ongoing training so advisors remain current on evolving standards. Ethics training is part of CFP Board's biennial 30-hour requirement.

The Big Six US regulations

Investment Advisers Act of 1940

Foundational framework for financial advisors. Advisors managing $100M+ register with SEC; smaller register at the state level. Imposes fiduciary obligations: act in client's best interests, avoid conflicts, provide comprehensive disclosure of fees and compensation. Prohibits fraudulent practices and false advertising.

Securities Act of 1933 — "Truth in Securities"

Ensures transparency in public securities offerings and protects investors from fraud. Mandates full disclosure through registration statements and prospectuses. Advisors must prioritize disclosure and verify information accuracy.

Securities Exchange Act of 1934

Established the SEC. Oversees securities trading and prevents fraudulent practices in secondary markets. Advisors must comply with SEC reporting standards and maintain ethical processes when executing transactions.

Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act of 2010

Comprehensive post-2008 reforms. Established the CFPB. Strengthened transparency, risk disclosure, and suitability requirements — especially for complex products marketed to non-institutional clients.

Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act of 1999 (GLBA)

Allowed financial institutions to combine banking, securities, and insurance operations — while imposing data privacy requirements. Advisors must protect client privacy and notify clients how personal financial information is handled.

AML & KYC obligations

Anti-Money Laundering laws require detection and reporting of suspicious deposit/withdrawal activity. Know Your Customer rules require client identity verification and risk-profile assessment. Together they ensure recommendations align with each client's documented goals, risk tolerance, time horizon, and experience.
