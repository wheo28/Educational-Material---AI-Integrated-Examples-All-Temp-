XI

Fair Treatment of Clients

Procedural fairness (how decisions are made), interactional fairness (how clients are treated), distributive fairness (how outcomes are allocated) — and the consumer-protection architecture and bias-intervention toolkit that operationalise all three.

Chapter Outcomes

  1. Explain the three core dimensions of fairness — procedural, interactional, and distributive — and the significance of each in service delivery.
  2. Describe procedural fairness principles: neutrality, transparency, adherence to due process.
  3. Analyse the role of courtesy, respect, attentiveness, and two-way communication in making clients feel valued and understood.
  4. Evaluate the equity of outcomes in financial transactions — fair allocation of resources, benefits, and costs based on contributions and needs.
  5. Discuss how relationship marketing, consumer protection laws, and regulatory frameworks promote fairness in client interactions.
  6. Identify methods to help clients overcome cognitive, behavioural, and psychological biases and decision paralysis.
Section i

Three dimensions · Two standards · One client

Use as your anchor

Chapter 11 builds the structural counterpart to Chapter 8's principles. Where Chapter 8 named what an advisor must BE (integrity, fairness, accountability, transparency), Chapter 11 names what fairness LOOKS like across three operational dimensions: procedural, interactional, and distributive. Behind it: relationship marketing as the philosophy, consumer protection law as the floor, and bias-aware practice as the daily discipline.

01
Procedural fairness
How decisions are made. Neutrality (consistent process for all clients). Transparency (each step visible and explainable). Due process (clients can raise concerns and have them addressed).
02
Interactional fairness
How clients are treated. Courtesy, respect, attentiveness, two-way communication. Did the client feel heard, valued, and understood? Interactional fairness is often what clients remember most about a relationship.
03
Distributive fairness
How benefits, costs, and resources are allocated. Equity of outcomes proportional to needs and contributions. Distributive fairness is where unfair pricing, hidden fees, share-class drift, and incentive conflicts surface most visibly.
04
Relationship marketing
The philosophical middle ground between "buyer beware" and "seller beware." Building meaningful long-term interactions to strengthen client satisfaction and loyalty. Emphasis on connection between provider and client — not the sale of a product.
05
Consumer protection laws
GLBA (privacy / NPI). Dodd-Frank → CFPB (consumer education + protection from deceptive practices). Older Americans Act (vulnerable-elder protection). FCRA / Fair and Accurate Credit Transaction Act. ECOA (anti-discrimination in credit). CARD Act. TILA. All four themes: disclosure, anti-discrimination, anti-deception, vulnerable-protection.
06
Bias-aware fair treatment
Eleven biases from Table 11.2 — anchoring, availability, bandwagon/herding, confirmation, emotional, framing, loss aversion, overconfidence, recency, status-quo, sunk-cost. Each can be exploited by misaligned advisors and each must be actively counteracted by ethical advisors.

The four practice standards (and the four themes in consumer-protection law)

Practice standards' four functions

(1) Identify acceptable practices — what is and is not within professional norms. (2) Foster internal control — codifying expectations within the firm. (3) Avoid confusion and reduce conflicts — by aligning client/firm/profession expectations. (4) Facilitate a basis for enforcement — without standards, regulators cannot define the rule that has been broken.

Disclosure (theme 1 across protection laws)

Truth in Lending Act, CARD Act, Fair Credit and Charge Card Disclosure Act, Consumer Leasing Act — all impose specific disclosure requirements. The principle: clients cannot make informed decisions without clear, accessible information about cost, terms, and risks.

Anti-discrimination (theme 2)

Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA) prohibits discrimination on grounds of sex, marital status, age, race, religion, colour, national origin, receipt of public assistance, or exercise of consumer-credit-protection rights. Community Reinvestment Act encourages financial institutions to serve low- and moderate-income communities.

Anti-deception (theme 3)

Dodd-Frank / CFPB enforcement of Unfair, Deceptive, or Abusive Acts and Practices (UDAAP) — across both bank and non-bank financial firms. Federal Trade Commission Act provisions on misleading advertising. The principle: even technically true statements can be deceptive in context.

Vulnerable-client protection (theme 4)

Older Americans Act — specific obligations to protect older individuals from financial abuse and scams. State-level vulnerable-adult and elder-abuse statutes. Provisions for under-21 consumers under the CARD Act. Trusted-contact rules under FINRA Rule 4512.

Communication preferences and fairness

Written, verbal, visual, tactile, digital, nonverbal — each preference has both a serving opportunity and a manipulation risk. Burying terms in fine print exploits written-preference clients; high-pressure phone scripts exploit verbal-preference clients; misleading visual design exploits visual learners. Match the channel — and audit the manipulation risk associated with each.
