Data Privacy & Cybersecurity

From GLBA to GDPR to DORA — and from Capital One's 100 million records to JPMorgan's 83 million accounts — the regulatory and threat architecture that defines what financial firms must protect, and how.

Chapter Outcomes

  1. Explain core requirements of major data protection regulations — GDPR, CCPA — and their impact on financial institutions.
  2. Describe robust data governance frameworks: secure handling, explicit consent, consumer-rights protection.
  3. Analyse ethical considerations to ensure confidentiality, integrity, and availability of sensitive client information.
  4. Demonstrate knowledge of advanced cybersecurity measures — encryption, multi-factor authentication, intrusion detection — to mitigate data privacy threats.
  5. Articulate the role of compliance with data privacy standards in maintaining trust, operational integrity, and strategic risk management.
Section i

CIA · Regulation · Threats · Human Factor

Use as your anchor

Chapter 10 frames data protection as fiduciary obligation, not IT problem. Three layers interlock: (1) the CIA model — confidentiality, integrity, availability — defining what must be protected; (2) the global regulatory architecture (US fragmented, EU integrated, Asia variable); (3) the cybersecurity threat typologies that target each CIA dimension. Behind all of them: the human factor — where most breaches actually originate.

01
Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability
The CIA triad is the foundation. Confidentiality: data accessible only to authorised parties. Integrity: data accurate and unaltered in transit or at rest. Availability: systems and data accessible when needed. Each cyberthreat targets a specific CIA dimension. Each control mitigates a specific dimension.
02
US fragmented regulatory landscape
Gramm-Leach-Bliley (GLBA) — fines up to $100,000 per violation. CCPA / CPRA — California privacy rights with no 30-day cure period since Jan 2023. Plus FFIEC, Federal Reserve guidance, PCI-DSS, NIST CSF, and state breach-notification laws. The fragmentation is itself a compliance challenge.
03
GDPR — the global gold standard
EU framework with extraterritorial scope. Penalties up to 4% of global turnover. Strict consent requirements. DORA (Jan 2025) standardises ICT risk management and incident reporting across EU financial firms. NIS2 expands cybersecurity obligations to critical sectors.
04
Asia-Pacific & Canada
Australia's Privacy Act 1988 (penalties raised 2024 to AU$50M+) + Notifiable Data Breaches scheme. Canada's CPPA: up to CA$25M or 5% of global turnover. China's PIPL/CSL/DSL — strict data localisation, fines up to 5% turnover or business suspension. Japan's APPI aligns with GDPR. Singapore's PDPA + Cybersecurity Act.
05
Threat typologies
Malware (viruses, worms, Trojans). Phishing & social engineering — exploiting human trust. DDoS (botnets overwhelming networks). Insider threats — privileged-access misuse, intentional or accidental. Each maps to a different CIA target and requires different controls.
06
The Human Factor
Most breaches originate not from technical sophistication but from human error. Phishing succeeds because people respond to fake urgency. Insider accidents happen because security gets in the way of speed. Cybersecurity culture, training, and behavioural design matter as much as encryption and MFA.
· · ·

Best practices: technical and organisational

Encryption

Data at rest (in databases, file systems, backups) and in transit (during transmission) must be encrypted with current standards. Encryption is the last line of defence: even if attackers exfiltrate data, encrypted files are unreadable without keys. Key management is itself a separate discipline.

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Single-factor (password-only) authentication is insufficient. MFA combines something you know (password), something you have (token, phone), and/or something you are (biometric). Mandatory for all employee access to systems handling client data, and increasingly mandatory for clients themselves.

Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS)

Network-based and host-based IDS monitor for suspicious patterns: unusual login times, unfamiliar geolocations, anomalous data-access volumes. Modern systems use AI to detect novel threats. Output feeds SIEM (Security Information and Event Management) for human review and response.

Vendor and third-party oversight

Cloud providers, SaaS tools, aggregators — all extend the firm's security perimeter. The 2023 Interagency Third-Party Guidance is explicit: third-party risk is the firm's risk. Required: due diligence at onboarding, contractual security obligations, ongoing monitoring, breach-notification clauses, exit-plan drills.

Incident response planning

Pre-defined playbook: containment, investigation, notification (regulatory + client), remediation, post-mortem. Tested through tabletop exercises and live drills. State breach-notification laws define specific timelines (often 72 hours under GDPR; varying under state laws). The plan must be ready BEFORE the breach.

Employee training and culture

Phishing simulations. Annual mandatory training with attestation. Anonymous reporting channels with anti-retaliation. Visible leadership engagement (board-level cybersecurity oversight). Without cultural alignment, technical controls fail because people circumvent them.
